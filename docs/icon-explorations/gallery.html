Riffs on the pinwheel

The current icon (left) stays the reference: six hand-placed cards, media glyphs, and the negative-space sparkle you like. Every exploration below keeps that DNA and answers one piece of your feedback — single-hue progressive shades, etched glyphs, new center shapes, dark mode throughout, and a layered Icon Composer rebuild.

Exploration A

Progressive shades, etched glyphs

One hue family per icon, six petals stepping light → deep clockwise from the blank top card. The glyphs are now debossed: tone-on-tone fills with a 1–2 px highlight/shadow fringe, so they read as pressed into the card — visible up close, invisible noise-free at home-screen size. The 64 px thumbnails under each tile are the honest small-size check.

Exploration B

What else can the center say?

Same pinwheel language, same ember ramp — but the petal tips are re-carved so the negative space forms a different mark. Nothing is drawn in the middle; the background genuinely shows through, exactly like the sparkle today.

B1Play button

Six petals whose seams land on the triangle’s vertices and side midpoints, keeping the corners sharp. Play is the universal “press to enjoy” of media — the strongest single symbol for an entertainment tracker. Unmistakable at 64 px.

B2Aperture

Six overlapping shutter blades form a hexagonal iris — the glyph-free, purely geometric variation. The shade-stepped overlaps create the swirl on their own; opening the iris is a nice metaphor for opening the app to see what’s next.

B3Rating star

Five petals at 72°, each tip carving one concave notch; the star’s points bleed into the seams exactly like the sparkle you already love. The app rates in half-stars, so the negative space now says “track and rate what you love.” The most legible of the set at small sizes.

B4Heart

Mirror-symmetric: the top petal’s tip splits into two prongs to own the cleft — the FedEx-arrow moment of the set — and the deepest shade wraps the point. Favourites made literal. Reads better in dark mode; if it advances, the ramp should start darker in light mode.

Exploration C

The Icon Composer rebuild

The icon ships today as one flat PNG. Rebuilt as a three-layer .icon file, iOS 26 renders it in Liquid Glass: specular edges that track device tilt, real depth behind the sparkle, and all four dynamic modes derived from one source. These mockups simulate how each mode should render — art direction, not pixel truth.

  • The sparkleis a hole in the petal layer, not a drawn shape. Default: carved into glass. Dark: inverts to a dark star. Clear: a literal window to the wallpaper. Tilting the phone parallaxes it deeper than the petals — for free.
  • Petal layerspecular on, soft neutral shadow, all six petals in one group so glass treats them as a single slab.
  • Glyph layerspecular off, shadow minimal — the layer’s parallax lift sells the deboss on its own.
  • Why one hue winsTinted mode maps luminance to the user’s tint, so a single-hue lightness ramp degrades perfectly to mono. The progressive-shades direction and the Composer direction reinforce each other.

Where I’d look first

  1. A1 Ember if the goal is refining the current identity — same warmth, mixed palette resolved into one ramp, sparkle untouched.
  2. B3 Rating star as the boldest evolution — closest kin to the sparkle, most legible at 64 px, and it means something (half-star ratings are core to the app).
  3. Exploration C is worth doing regardless of which flat design wins: any of these variants can be split into the same three layers, and Tinted/Clear modes are exactly where the single-hue ramp pays off.
